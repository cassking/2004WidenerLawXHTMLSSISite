Job Search: Judicial Clerkships

Judicial clerkships can be an excellent way to gain valuable legal research and writing experience, potentially establish a mentorship relationship with a judge, and learn about various substantive areas of the law and procedural rules. If you are interested, be sure to familiarize yourself with the application procedures for each state.

For an overview of judicial clerkship requirements for the various courts, you can simply click on the state(s) of your choice. But before you do, please take a minute to review the following:

Important: In March of 2002,an Ad Hoc Committee, co-chaired by the Hon. Edward R. Becker and the Hon. Harry Edwards, reported that federal appellate judges voted overwhelmingly in favor of a new Plan for law clerk hiring. The Plan includes:

  1. A moratorium on law clerk hiring during the fall of 2002;
  2. An arrangement ensuring that the hiring of law clerks will not be done earlier than the fall of the third or fourth year of law school (beginning on the Tuesday after Labor Day); and
  3. An agreement that focuses law clerk hiring on third or fourth year law students.

The U.S. District Courts in PA, DE and NJ have agreed to abide by the same hiring plan. Students interested in other districts are encouraged to check the website http://www.cadc.uscourts.gov/lawclerk/ or contact those Courts directly for current hiring information.

The following information was compiled from a variety of sources and is intended to be used as a general guide only. Since judges usually determine individually if and when they will accept clerkship applications, as well as what application materials must be submitted, students should confirm application dates and procedures with the judges with whom they intend to apply early in the process. Please check The Digest and Career Connection for changes and updates. In addition, some judges advertise positions with the Career Development Office. The staff of the Career Development Office recommend early application and are eager to assist students in their search for judicial clerkships. Please remember that if you wait until the "deadline" to apply to a particular judge, you may have missed an excellent opportunity.
