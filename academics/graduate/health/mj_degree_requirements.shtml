Master of Jurisprudence (M.J.) in Health Law: Curriculum, Academic & Degree Requirements

The M.J. in Health Law is a part-time, evening program for the working professional. The program is scheduled to take two academic years, including one required summer of study. Candidates are required to complete a total of 30 semester hours of coursework at the graduate level. Twenty-three of these hours are required courses, including a master's thesis. The remaining seven credit hours are health law electives. Graduate students must complete all degree requirements within four years of matriculation. Credits earned in the M.J. program are not transferable to the J.D. program.

Introductory Summer Term and First Year

All M.J. candidates must take M.J. Introduction to Law (2 credits), which is offered during the summer before the first full academic year. This course lays the groundwork for further legal studies. During the fall semester of their first year, M.J. students take Torts (4 credits) and Legal Methods (3 credits), and the following spring they take Constitutional Law (3 credits) and Business/Adminstrative Law (3 credits). The introductory summer course and first year M.J. courses meet two nights per week. (Many M.J. students take courses during the summer term after their first year to reduce the credit load during their second year.)

Second Year

After their first year, M.J. students are required to take Health Law I (formerly Health Care Liability & Quality, 3 credits) and Health Care Regulation (2 or 3 credits). These two courses are typically taken during the second year. In addition, all M.J. candidates must take Health Law Thesis I and Health Law Thesis II. Through these seminars, students will write a Master's Thesis. The seminars will provide each student with the research and mentoring support for production of a publishable work of advanced scholarship.
