Health Law: Required Core Courses

  • Advanced Forensic Evidence (2 credits) This is an advanced seminar in evidence, with primary emphasis on cutting edge scientific developments. The seminar is taught by an experienced trial lawyer and a Ph.D. scientist, with several guest lecturers.
  • Bioethics and the Law (2 credits) This seminar examines ethical issues involved in the delivery of health care, considering such topics as advanced medical technology at the end of life and advanced medical directives.
  • Biotechnology and the Law (2 credits) Biotechnology is revolutionizing health care and is at the forefront of research and development ("R&D) for global industries ranging from agriculture to industrial processes. The course will explore this technology and a range of areas in law, business, and bioethics along the R&D continuum from the laboratory bench to the marketplace.
  • Children's Health: Legal, Policy, and Ethical Issues (2 credits) This course provides a detailed examination of the legal and ethical issues relating to children's health with particular focus on the right to demand treatment, right of parents to refuse treatment (Jehovah Witnesses' cases), right to die, an encephalic infants as organ donors, access to medical treatment for poor children, pediatric drug research, and teenagers and reproductive rights.
  • ERISA This course surveys the federal taxation and regulation of pension and employee benefit plans. Some provisions of the Internal Revenue Code an the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) will be examined in depth, and the application of other laws such as the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA) will be considered. Central themes from the course include the various types of tax-qualified pension plans and deferred compensation arrangements, welfare benefits, administrative requirements and fiduciary roles and responsibilities.
  • Food and Drug Law (2 credits) This seminar provides a detailed look at the regulatory system that governs food, drugs, medical devices, and cosmetics. The course also examines product liability issues associated with the marketing of these products.
  • Health Care Antitrust (2 credits) This course examines the application of the antitrust laws to the health care industry. It includes consideration of issues arising from agreements and collective action among health care providers, such as agreements related to fees and accreditation activities. The course also addresses the restructuring and integration of the health care industry, including issues raised by joint ventures among physicians, hospital mergers, and financial arrangements between providers and third-party payers.
  • Health Care Finance* (2 credits) This course examines how health care services rendered by physicians and institutional providers are reimbursed. The course provides the student with an understanding of the complex interaction of private insurance, the federal Medicare program and the state/federal Medicaid program. *This course is required for all LL.M. students.

Health Care Industry Courses

  • Regulating Fraud and Abuse (2 credits) This seminar provides a sophisticated analysis of various statutes and other sources of law that regulate physician and provider conflicts of interest, including the fraud and abuse amendments, Stark II, the Clinical Laboratories Act, and other state and federal laws.
  • Health Care Liability and Quality* (3 credits) This course provides a detailed introduction to the legal issues created by the provider-patient relationship. Topics include risk management and tort liability as applied to health care delivery, the trade-offs between cost and quality in health care, public and private regulation of the industry, patient rights, and other issues in the acute care setting. The course examines specific problem areas such as medical technology and new experimental therapies as windows into many of these topics. *This course is required for all LL.M. and M.J. students.
  • Health Care Profession (2 credits) This seminar is designed to acquaint law students with the medical profession: the doctor-patient relationship and the emergence of managed care; and the physician's relationship to institutional providers such as hospital and integrated delivery systems.
  • Health Care Regulation* (2 credits) A detailed examination of health care as a regulated industry, this course looks at cost controls and promotion of access to health care through private and public law. It provides a look at the history of American health care, the policies behind various reimbursement programs such as Medicare and Medicaid, and the regulatory strategies developed to control costs (DRGs, fraud and abuse laws, etc.) and to promote access (EMTALA, tax exemptions). Topics considered include hospital operation, managed care organizations, and physician practices within this highly regulated environment. *This course is required for all LL.M. and M.J. students.
  • Health Care Transactions (2 credits) This course provides an overview of the rapidly evolving organizational forms through which health care is delivered in the United States and examines contemporary health care transactions, such as the formation of integrated delivery systems, physician practice management companies, physician practice acquisitions and physician compensation. The issues are reviewed from a transactional lawyer's perspective, and case studies are used to identify relevant legal issues and to arrive at practical legal solutions.

Health Law Externship

  • Veterans' Assistance Program (2 credits) This externship handles veterans' benefits cases and discharge review cases. Students' interview and counsel clients, prepare discharge review petitions, do legal research and assist in writing appellate briefs. The classroom component meets once a week for two hours. Suitable for extended division students as well as day students, externs are expected to spend at least four hours a week working on Veterans' Assistance Program projects at the law school under the supervision of Professor Tom Reed. Prerequisites are evidence and Professional Responsibility.
  • Health Law Graduate Thesis Seminar I (1 credit)
  • Health Law Graduate Thesis Seminar II (2 credits) These seminars will provide each graduate health law student with the research and mentoring support needed for the production of a publishable work of advanced scholarship. During Seminar I, the student must determine a thesis topic acceptable to the faculty advisor, begin the research, and complete a detailed outline of the paper. During Seminar II, the student must submit drafts of the paper for comment and complete the final draft. Students must then defend their papers orally to the faculty Health law Committee.
  • HIV/AIDS and the Law (2 credits) This seminar is a study of the problems faced by persons with AIDS as part of a larger look at the role of public health in our society. It looks at how the law can protect persons who are HIV-positive or are diagnosed with AIDS against discrimination in housing, employment, insurance and health care.
  • Law and Psychology (2 credits) This seminar explores mental illness and mental retardation in various contexts, such as criminal law, institutions, and the community, and torts and employment.
  • Law and the Elderly (2 credits) This seminar addresses such topics as living wills and other advanced directives, Medicare, Medicaid eligibility, estate planning, reverse mortgages, and guardianships. Students also learn practice techniques for working with older clients.

  • Managed Care Organizations

  • Legal Issues (2 credits) This seminar looks at the history, development and current structure of managed care organizations, with particular attention to financing and how physician practice is affected. Issues of consumer rights, professional liability, and quality assurance are considered.
  • Medical Malpractice (2 credits) This course will explore the various theories of physician liability, hospital liability, HMO liability and pharmaceutical liability. Included among the bases of liability will be informed consent, ostensible agency, negligent credentialing of physicians, nursing negligence and negligent referral. Practice issues relating to evidence of the standard of care, discovery, deposition taking and trial preparation will also be covered.
  • Professional Liability and Insurance (2 credits) This course analyzes liability of physicians, attorneys, architects, accountants, insurance brokers, and others. Approximately one-half of course time is devoted to medical malpractice including hospital and corporate liability. The materials and lectures attempt to provide a basic understanding for the handling of a medical-legal or other professional liability case. The grounds of liability, the applicable standard of care, statutes of limitations, burden of proof, and damages are among the topics analyzed. Some time is spent in analyzing the unique terms, elements and issues involved in professional insurance coverage.
  • Public Health and the Law (2 credits) Legislatures, state and federal agencies, and courts charged with protecting the public health are governed by requirements of law: constitutional, statutory and regulatory, and common. In a variety of contexts, this course explores the responsibility, actions and decisions of these entities in their efforts to safeguard the public health. This course explores the public health response to the problems of infectious diseases, chronic illnesses, accidents, and death and dying. The state's power to encroach on the liberties and decision making of individuals where it is argued to serve a greater, public good will be considered. The course may also explore such measures as civil commitment, procreation, and human experimentation and clinical research.
  • Reproductive Rights and the Law (2 credits) This seminar examines the development of laws affecting reproduction, including sterilization, contraception, abortion, and maternal-fetal conflicts. It explores technological advances that have created new legal challenges in these areas, including in vitro fertilization, genetic testing, and the impact of the human genome project.
  • Representing the Elderly Client (2 credits) This course will address such topics as ethical issues involved in representing older clients, living wills and other advance directives, elder abuse, nursing homes and other housing alternatives, Medicare, Medicaid eligibility, estate planning, reverse mortgages and guardianship.
  • "Right to Die" and Other End-Of-Life Decisions (2 credits) Ten years ago, the U.S. Supreme Court's Cruzan opinion established that individuals have a right to decline life-saving medical treatment even if death will clearly ensue. Cruzan, subsequent state court opinions, and federal and state statutes involving end-of-life medical treatment have had a profound impact on the way in which end-of-life care is delivered. Such decisions are necessary under extremely varied situations - from a parent's decision to withhold treatment of a premature newborn, to a cancer patient's decision to utilize physician-assisted suicide, to a daughter's decision to make her elderly mother "DNR." This course uses a fiction novel and other popular media articles to form a basis for class discussion addressing the legal, ethical and practical impact of these and other aspects of the right to die.
  • Selected Topics in Euthanasia (2 credits) This seminar examines the legal and ethical issues spawned by the topic of euthanasia. Legal decisions, statutes and ethics articles on the topics are analyzed. The class explores the withholding of treatment, assisted suicide, and do-not-resuscitate orders for elderly patients.
  • Tobacco and the Law (2 credits) This seminar will focus on the many and varied legal developments involving the tobacco industry, both litigation issues and regulatory matters, incorporating current events into the study material. The grade for the course will be based primarily on a series of small "thought" papers directed at the reading assignments. Note: Not a writing requirement course. Some topics that may be discussed include: cultural history of the tobacco industry; the role of risk assessment in tobacco law; smoker product liability litigation; secondhand smoke litigation and regulation; other litigation (lawsuits by states for reimbursement of Medicaid funds expended on smoking-related illnesses; insurers' lawsuits for reimbursement); may the FDA regulate nicotine?; the political climate; the recent "proposed settlements"; tobacco advertising and marketing.
  • Toxic Torts (3 credits) The rapid growth of hazardous substances litigation raises unique legal questions. This course examines both the substantive and procedural problems related to toxic tort litigation, with emphasis on toxic product litigation and hazardous waste. Subject areas include: product liability; clean-up of hazardous waste sites; occupational exposures; causation and scientific evidence; new theories (e.g., claims for increased risk of disease); government liability; and mass torts. Substances addressed may include asbestos, DES and other prescription drugs, chemical exposures, cigarettes, blood products, radiation, lead paint, pesticides, and silicone gel breast implants.
