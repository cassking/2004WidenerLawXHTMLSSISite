  • What documentation is required for admission into the program?

    Answer Seven:     A letter of good standing from the student's home law school is required for processing the application. The letter should specifically state that the applicant has, or will have, completed one full year of law studies by the time the program takes place. The letter should also specifically state that the student is IN GOOD STANDING at his/her home law school. While no specific grade point average is required to attend any of Widener's institutes, as a general rule a G.P.A. of 2.3 or above is acceptable.

    Note: Students on academic probation, or, students who believe they may be placed on academic probation during the semester preceding the summer institute may attend the program on a case-by-case basis, and do so AT THEIR OWN RISK. There have, unfortunately, been cases where students have been dismissed during a program because of their grades from a previous semester.

    A fully completed application (with all information for each question answered), as well as the letter of good standing must be forwarded to the Coordinator for International Programs. ÊNo application fee is required; however, accepted students must pay a non-refundable fee of $50 by April 15, 2004.

  • How many courses can I take? Will credit for the course(s) be applicable at my home institution? Will participation in a Widener Summer Program accelerate my graduation?

    Answer Nine:     All Widener summer programs allow for a maximum of (6) credits, or (2) courses to be taken during a the program.

    Widener courses may be credited toward your law degree at an ABA-approved institution if your law school accepts credits for summer programs. A transcript of grades will be sent to each student and to their home school. Acceptance of any credit or grade for any course is subject to determination by the student's home school.

    It is unlikely that participation in a foreign summer program may be used to accelerate graduation. Consult with your law school's registrar

  • What health precautions are required or necessary for travel to Kenya?

    Answer Seventeen:    There are no required inoculations to enter Kenya. You will be staying in a safe apartment building and in luxury accommodations in the game parks where it is highly unlikely that you will be even exposed to serious tropical diseases. Yet it is wise and I recommend that you take inoculations for yellow fever and typhoid/diptheria. They are available at many university health centers and major city hospitals. It is also wise to update your tetanus shots. Malaria is a potential problem, but precautions can be taken to minimize this risk. In 15 years of the program no student has gotten malaria. Malaria is caused by the parasite in the saliva of the female Anopheles mosquito that bites in the night or early morning hours before dawn. These mosquitos are rarely found in Nairobi at 5500 feet particularly when you will be there. June, July and August are sometimes called the Kenyan 'winter' because it is colder than other times, but the temperature will be about 70 during the day and 50s at night. There is some risk on the coast where it is in the 80s and other lowland areas. There are two precautions: first, avoid being bitten and second, take an anti-malarial drug. You are unlikely to be bitten if you put on deet at night when outside and wear long pants at night. If you sleep in an air conditioned room on the coast as we all do and use it, then you will not be bitten in your room. Second, there are a number of choices of anti-malarial drugs.

    You might check the CDC website (http://www.cdc.gov/travel/malariadrugs.htm) for a discussion of the merits of different drugs. The good news is that there is a new drug, Malarone that is quite effective and side effects are very rare.
